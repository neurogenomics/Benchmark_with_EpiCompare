Lay Summary

Cells in the human body number in the trillions and all carry the identical genome. Beyond the primary sequence of the genome, epigenetic modifications regulate the expression of different genes and give birth to cells of all shapes and sizes. In cells, DNA is coiled many times around proteins called histones. Modifications on these histones could regulate the expression of certain genes, for instance, by affecting the nearby DNA regulatory regions which do not code any protein but monitor certain gene expression. Dysregulation of these histone modifications, however, is connected with various diseases. Thus, mapping these modifications to the specific genome region is imperative in understanding their role in disease development.

Histone modifications were originally profiled with a technique called chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP), yet it is lagged due to various problems such as the requirement of large numbers of cell input and low signal-to-noise ratio. In 2017, Skene and Henikoff have come up with a new epigenetic profiling method called Cleavage Under Targets & Release Using Nuclease (CUT&RUN), which overcomes some of the problems of ChIP. Though they have claimed the superiority of this method in mapping transcriptional factors binding regions, the performance of this new method has not been benchmarked (comparison with the standard references) in profiling histone modification in K562 cell line (a model cell line of human lymphoblasts) yet.

A preliminary benchmark of CUT&RUN of such histone modifications was performed by Yang from the neurogenomics lab. Nevertheless, such benchmark of CUT&RUN against the de facto golden standard ChIP-seq reference, the Encyclopedia of DNA Elements (ENCODE), lacks a baseline for a robust comparison. As a follow-up, this study examined the inherent robustness of the ENCODE ChIP-seq reference datasets first and set the inherent overlap as a baseline for CUT&RUN benchmark. It is revealed that the overlap between ENCODE references was comparatively low, and few histone modifications profiled with CUT&RUN were consistent with the references. These results throw light on the future benchmarking and technical optimization of new epigenetic profiling strategies.

Abstract

Genome-wide epigenetic profiles are originally carried out with chromatin immunoprecipitation followed by next generation sequencing (ChIP-seq) to understand the regulatory mechanism of gene expression. As an emerging alternative to ChIP-seq, the Cleavage Under Targets & Release Using Nuclease (CUT&RUN), as performed in situ, has shown a high-resolution chromatin mapping with low cell input. Nevertheless, an integrative analysis and benchmarking against the published ChIP-seq datasets are not yet performed. Here, the performance of CUT&RUN was tested on 11 histone modifications of the K562 cell line against the de facto golden standard ChIP-seq datasets, the Encyclopedia of DNA Elements (ENCODE). However, an unexpected and partially modification-dependent low reproducibility was revealed between the ENCODE datasets, indicating the inherent inconsistency of the reference ChIP-seq datasets. By filtering out the unconvincing references, data derived from CUT&RUN were shown to have little overlap with ENCODE datasets on most of the histone modifications. This study analyses and benchmarks CUT&RUN against the ChIP-seq reference datasets, setting the cornerstone for future epigenetic profiling and technique optimization.

Keywords: Epigenomics, chromatin immunoprecipitation and next generation sequencing (ChIP-seq), post-translational modifications (PTMs), Cleavage Under Targets & Release Using Nuclease (CUT&RUN), Encyclopedia of DNA Elements (ENCODE), peaks

  • Introduction

Introduction

Human genome has been completely sequenced for more than two decades (1), laying a solid foundation for the subsequent biomedical research (2). The preservation of the primary sequence among all human cell types raises an intriguing question on how the identical genome copies give rise to more than 200 different types of human cells identified so far. Beyond the order of ATGC nucleotides, a plethora of evidence has been found on the spatial- and temporal-specific regulatory effect of epigenome on gene expression, revealing a “second dimension to the genome” (3). While much emphasis has been placed on profiling the gene expression dynamics on pathways and networks from the perspective of systems biology (4), the cis-regulatory elements remain uncharted for a comprehensive epigenomic landscape (5).

Given the dominance of chromatin dynamics in controlling DNA accessibility and mediating regulatory signals, a genome-wide chromatin profile is the premise of a systematic map of the cis-regulatory elements (5). Specifically, there is overwhelming evidence indicating the close correlation between histone post-translational modifications (PTMs) (Figure 1) and the activity of promoters, enhancers (6), regulator binding, transcription initiation (7), and repression as well (8). Activity of these cis-regulatory elements could be further tuned by the orchestration of multiple histone modifications (9). Disharmony of these histone modifications, however, plays a significant role in disease pathogenesis. For instance, H3K27ac and H3K9ac are highly cell type-specific histone H3 modifications and are preferential marks of active regulatory regions, namely, promoters and enhancers (10)(11). Gains of these histone modifications were linked to transcription, chromatin and disease pathways in Alzheimer's disease (AD) (12). Given the inherent heterogeneity in disease organs and tissues, an integrative cell type-specific epigenomic annotation is thus imperative in deciphering the disease pathogenesis.






Profiling of histone modifications is most commonly carried out with chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP) (13), followed by next generation sequencing (ChIP-seq). In the ChIP-seq protocol, sample cells are first cross-linked with formaldehyde. Chromatin is then fragmented and solubilized, where an antibody is added to bind and precipitate the targeted chromatin. DNA is then extracted from the recovered antibody-bound chromatin for sequencing. Nevertheless, ChIP-seq and its recent variations (14)(15) were reported to lag owing to the low signal-to-noise ratio and epitope masking due to cross-linking, and the demand of a considerable amount of cell samples due to their low yields (16)(17). To overcome these problems, an in situ method termed “Cleavage Under Targets & Release Using Nuclease (CUT&RUN)” was proposed by Skene and Henikoff in 2017 to map the protein-DNA interaction (18)(19) (Figure 2). In CUT&RUN, the endo-exonuclease Micrococcal Nuclease (MNase) is tethered to the targeted protein in situ by the antibody and the chimeric fusion with Protein A (pA-MN). The targeted protein-DNA complexes are then cleaved by MNase (calcium-controlled activity) and released into the supernatant, with DNA fragments extracted and sequenced.




In mapping the transcriptional factors (TFs) binding sites, Skene and Henikoff have reported a high resolution, low background noise with the requirement of only one tenth sequencing depth as ChIP-seq for the CUT&RUN strategy (18). Yet the performance of this novel alternative to ChIP is not further explored in profiling histone modifications. A preliminary benchmark of CUT&RUN strategy in histone profiling was performed by Yang (20) from the neurogenomics lab. Nevertheless, such benchmark of CUT&RUN against the de facto golden standard ChIP-seq reference, the Encyclopedia of DNA Elements (ENCODE), lacks a baseline for a robust comparison. Here, the inherent overlaps between the ENCODE references were first tested and set as the baseline for an integrative analysis and benchmark of CUT&RUN in chromatin profiling.

  • Methods

Methods

  • Samples preparation and sequencing
  • Sequence data process with nf-core/cutandrun pipeline
  • Peaks comparison and functional analysis with EpiCompare

For the data collection, 11 PTMs were tested on the human lymphoblastoid cell line K-562 with modified CUT&RUN protocol (19) for efficient isolation of the targeted nuclei. The digested DNA fragments were then amplified with PCR with their size distribution checked. These fragments were then sequenced with pair-end Illumina sequencing protocol. Libraries were pooled to equally represent the desired final library size range. All these referred wet lab works were performed by Yang from the neurogenomics lab and were detailed in her thesis (20).

A standardised pipeline for processing the CUT&RUN sequence was proposed by Cheshire and West (21). This nextflow-based (22) nf-core/cutandrun pipeline was then slightly modified and used for analysing and interpreting the sequenced CUT&RUN data on Imperial's High Performance Computing (HPC). Briefly, in the nf-core/cutandrun pipeline, the raw CUT&RUN sequencing reads were first trimmed with the low-quality reads and adaptors removed. The trimmed FastQ files were aligned to human genome assembly GRCh38 (hg38) with Bowtie2 (23). Potential PCR duplicates were then removed. Enriched genomic regions (termed “peaks”) were called (filtered) by SEACR (Sparse Enrichment Analysis for CUT&RUN, v1.4) in a stringent manner with a previously optimised cut-off (with a false discovery rate at 1X10-2) (24) to reduce the false-positive peaks. (click MultiQC to see MultiQC report)

The ChIP-seq derived datasets by the Encyclopedia of DNA Elements (ENCODE) consortium have been referred to as the de facto gold standard for the profiling of multiple epigenetic modifications. To further benchmark the CUT&RUN data against the ENCODE data, the peak overlap between the ENCODE files on the experiment level: with ENCODE data from different experiments, indicating the potential outcome difference between different protocols in sample procession and downstream analysis; and replicates level: biological (isogenic) and technical (pseudoreplicate) replicates, indicating the potential variation in the specific experiment. The overlap between the different ENCODE files for the same histone modification was referred to as the baseline for evaluating the performance of CUT&RUN. The CUT&RUN data were then benchmarked against the corresponding ENCODE reference. Peak overlap between the ENCODE references and CUT&RUN data was calculated by EpiCompare, an R package developed by Choi et al., 2022 from the neurogenomics lab. In EpiCompare, peaks are then mapped to their proximal genes with ChIPseeker, v1.32.0 (25). Additionally, peaks are assigned and annotated to the ChromHMM-derived chromatin states (26) with genomation, v1.28.0 (27). Further functional annotation was performed and visualized with clusterProfiler (28) for gene ontology (GO) enrichment analysis (29) and Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes (KEGG) pathway analysis (30). The EpiCompare used here is v1.0.0 with the parameters set as: genome_build = "hg38”, blacklist = hg38_blacklist (31), chromHMM_annotation = "K562".

Code and data availability: Bash scripts for no-core/cutandrun pipeline. Template R script for genome-wide histone modification comparison with EpiCompare. Detailed R scripts and markdowns for data load and EpiComapre comparison.

  • Results

Results

The raw sequencing data of both ChIP-seq and CUT&RUN were processed with a standard pipeline respectively. Fragments were aligned to the reference genome, uncovering the “peaks” (enriched genomic regions). The overlaps of peaks in the de facto golden standard ChIP-seq reference, the ENCODE, were first tested to 1) construct a standard pipeline for benchmarking the alternative epigenomic profiling strategy; 2) examine the inherent reproducibility and reliability of the reference; and 3) set a comparable baseline for CUT&RUN benchmarking. Both inter- (profiling of the same histone modifications across different ENCODE experiments) and intra- (biological and technical replicates, also different ENCODE annotation and genome assembly versions) experiment overlaps were compared. Due to the limited numbers of ENCODE experiments in histone modification of K562 cell lines, the inter-experiment reproducibility of histone modification ENCODE references were first examined on A549 cell lines (human lung carcinoma epithelial cells). The number of peaks identified on A549 H3K4me3 is consistent across a series of ENCODE experiments, and this is also the case for A549 H3K9me3 (Figure 3A, 3B). Similarly, the narrow distribution of peak width indicates the consistency of peak width across different ENCODE experiments for both A549 H3K4me3 and A549 H3K9me3 (Figure 3C, 3D). Interestingly, though the pairwise peak overlap of A549 H3K4me3 shows an overall high correlation, the pairwise peak overlap of A549 H3K9me3 is comparatively low (Figure 4), implying the inherent reproducibility and reliability of the golden standard ENCODE references may not be as robust as expected.






To further examine the ENCODE reference consistency on the intra-experiment level (also inter-experiment), and to set a comparable baseline for benchmarking, most of the available ENCODE references of 11 K562 histone modifications (all the histone modifications mapped with CUT&RUN) were first compared. For modifications with multiple available ENCODE experiments (H3K4me1, H3K9ac, H3K27me3 and H3K36me3), variations in peak number and peak width are non-negligible (Supplementary figure S1, S2). Differences in peak number, in turn, affect the calculation of z-score for peak overlap, even though H3K27me3 shows a comparatively high overlap (Figure 5 Top; see overlap for H3K4me1, H3K9ac and H3K36me3). Intriguingly, similar to the results in A549, K562 H3K4me3 shows a high peak overlap (as well as the peak number and peak width) across 4 available ENCODE experiments, while K562 H3K9me3 shows a comparatively low peak overlap within the only one available ENCODE experiment (significant variation in peak number and peak width) (Figure 5 Middle and Bottom). The intra-experiment ENCODE consistency was further checked on the left K562 histone modifications. Similarly, except for H3K4me2 and H3K27ac, most of the isogenic (biological) replicates within ENCODE experiments do not have a high overlap with each other (see in H3K4me2, H3K9me1, H3K4me2, H3K79me2, H4K20me1 ).

  • Discussion

Discussion

Here an integrative analysis and benchmark of CUT&RUN chromatin profiling strategy were conducted against the ENCODE ChIP-seq datasets. The inherent consistency of ENCODE ChIP-seq datasets was first tested and set as a baseline for the benchmark. Unexpectedly, it is found that for some of the histone modifications, the variation in peak overlap of inter- and intra-experiment ENCODE datasets is non-negligible, doubting the reliability of the ENCODE ChIP-seq references. Further benchmark of CUT&RUN data was then carried out against robust (high peak overlap) ENCODE references. Except for H3K27me3, CUT&RUN profiled a quite different histone modification landscape compared to the ENCODE references in peak overlap and functional annotation, indicating the reproducibility and reliability of the CUT&RUN strategy in profiling histone modification may be dependent on the type of modification.

For years, ENCODE datasets generated by ChIP-seq have served as the golden standard references for epigenetic profiling (32). Multiple emerging alternatives to ChIP-seq are preferentially benchmarked against the ENCODE references to test their reliability. For instance, Hu from neurogenomics lab has benchmarked another enzyme tethering strategy, CUT&Tag (Cleavage Under Targets & Tagmentation), against ENCODE for H3K27ac, and reported an up to 50% peak recovery (24). Surprisingly, by comparing the pairwise peak overlap between ENCODE datasets, it is noticed that the inherent reproducibility and reliability of ENCODE dataset are not as robust as expected. The non-negligible irreproducibility of the ENCODE datasets causes further uncertainty in CUT&RUN benchmarking when taken as the references. To further reveal the performance of CUT&RUN (also other emerging epigenetic profiling methods), the generated peaks should be compared to 1) peaks generated by other similar enzyme tethering methods (such as CUT&Tag); 2) peaks generated by non-enzyme tethering methods; and finally 3) multiple ENCODE reference datasets. Also, a statistical model should be constructed to evaluate the significance of both pairwise and overall peak overlap statistically.

Given the fact that ChIP-seq (ENCODE references) and CUT&RUN strategy are both immuno methods, and their robustness might be partially modification-dependent, the specificity of the primary antibody (most of the primary antibodies used in CUT&RUN were same as those used in ENCODE ChIP-seq) requires further examination. Nevertheless, irrespective of problems with ENCODE datasets, CUT&RUN still profiles quite different peaks compared to ENCODE reference. Of note, the aforementioned benchmark was all conducted with limited CUT&RUN data with similar experiment settings and processed with the same pipeline. To further consolidate these results, improvements should be made in experiment design, CUT&RUN protocol and data process pipeline. For the experiment design, both biological and technical replicates should be included for each histone modification to reveal the intra-experiment reproducibility of CUT&RUN. As an emerging method, although CUT&RUN has shown good performance in mapping transcriptional factors, many steps of the original CUT&RUN protocol require further optimization. In CUT&Tag, Hu has shown that experiment settings such as antibody dilution ratio, DNA extraction methods, PCR cycle number may all affect the final output (24).
